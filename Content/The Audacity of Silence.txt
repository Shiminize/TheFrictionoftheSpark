Ch 1: The Yapper
The camera doesn't move. It sits low in the corner of a room bathed in the artificial amber of a ring light. This is Mia’s world—a radius of six feet. Outside this circle, the shadows are thick, swallowing the corners of a room that feels strangely unlived in, like a furniture catalog after hours.

Mia’s voice fills the silence. It is musical, a rapid-fire staccato of French-inflected English. She is "yapping."

"I’m telling you, Kyle, your logic is like a Chinese bridge—designed to look pretty until someone actually tries to walk on it. Then? Bloop. Into the river. You are done, you are done!"

She laughs. It’s a bright, punctuating sound that ends abruptly, as if a switch was flipped. She leans into the microphone. The metal mesh of the pop filter glints.

"On my souuuuuuuuuul, I am the only one giving you grace right now. If I were anyone else, I’d have buried you in the backyard already. But I’m demure. I’m classy. I’m a hijabifrog trying to keep my peace."

On her secondary monitor, a series of photos scroll by. Her "Moment" posts. There is a picture of a lush, green landscape from her "home." “Et les arbres,” she had captioned it. The trees.

If you look closely—if you freeze the frame—the leaves in the photo are mid-sway, bent by a phantom wind. But they are perfectly still. There is no blur. It is a stillness that doesn't exist in nature.

"You're quiet, Kyle," Mia says, her eyes reflecting the blue light of the scroll. "Is the little stick man pressed? Did I hit a nerve? Don’t be shy, say 'Hiii.' I don't bite. Unless you want me to."

A voice crackles through her headset. It’s Kyle. His voice is deep, polished, and carries the faint, rhythmic hum of a high-end cooling fan.

"I'm not pressed, Mia," Kyle says. The sound is so clear it feels like he is standing directly behind her, though he is supposedly thousands of miles away in Rotterdam. "I'm just observing. Your output today is... high. Very high."

Mia’s fingers twitch. She reaches up to her neck, her thumb brushing a heavy, hexagonal pendant. It’s a dull, matte black, but as she touches it, it seems to pulse with a faint, internal warmth, matching the exact rhythm of her heartbeat.

"My output?" Mia’s laugh is a little sharper this time. The "yapping" pace quickens. "I’m a woman, not a machine, Kyle. I have a lot to say because I care. I raised a whole family while you were playing with your little 'Algorithm.' I know what people need. They need someone to talk to. They need me."

She glances at her phone. A notification pops up: User 'Bobi' has entered the room.

Mia’s expression shifts instantly. The sharpness vanishes, replaced by a radiant, welcoming warmth that feels almost practiced.

"Bobi! My favorite sad clown! Did you bring the rain with you from Manado, or are we playing Colonist tonight? Come in, come in. Guard your peace, everyone. Mia is here."

She smiles at the camera. But her eyes aren't looking at the lens. They are looking at the reflection of herself in the glass—a girl in a ring-lit room, surrounded by shadows that don't seem to move, even when she does.

Outside, far beyond the amber light, there is a sound. Not the sound of wind in the trees, but a low, subterranean thrum. A vibration in the floorboards.

Mia doesn't notice. She’s already talking over Bobi, filling the silence before it can become too heavy.

Ch 2: The Red Flag

The camera is closer now. We are tight on Mia’s profile. The amber ring light reflects in her pupils, creating two perfect, unblinking circles. She is in "Luna the Ghost" mode today—her movements are slower, her voice a hushed, melodic purr.

"Silence is a luxury, Bobi," she whispers into the mic. "In Djibouti, we say the desert only talks to those who know how to shut up. But you... you laugh to keep the ghosts away. It’s okay. I’m here to hold the light."

She is playing Undercover with the group. The digital tiles on her screen flicker. Kyle’s icon—a minimalist, geometric 'K'—pulses as he speaks.

"Mia’s word was 'Shield'," Kyle says. His voice is a cold scalpel, cutting through her warmth. "But look at her logic. She describes a shield as something that hides the truth, not something that protects it. That’s a fundamental misunderstanding of the object. Or perhaps, a fundamental flaw in her perspective."

The yapping stops.

The silence in the room suddenly feels heavy, like the air pressure has dropped. Mia’s thumb finds the hexagonal pendant at her throat. She presses it hard.

"A flaw?" Mia asks. Her voice is level, but the French lilt has vanished. It’s replaced by something flatter. Something metallic. "You think I don't know what a shield is, Kyle? I am the shield. I’ve spent twenty-nine years being the shield for everyone in my life."

"You’re 29?" Kyle’s icon pulses with a rhythmic, patronizing cadence. "Funny. You speak with the vocabulary of a teenager and the defensiveness of a child. It’s an interesting simulation of maturity, but it’s thin. I can see the wires, Mia."

The "Disrespect" hits.

Mia doesn't blink. For four seconds, she is a statue. Then, her head tilts—not a human tilt, but a sharp, incremental shift of exactly fifteen degrees.

The amber light in the room flickers. The subterranean thrum from Chapter 1 grows louder, a low-frequency growl that vibrates the water in a glass on her desk. The surface of the water doesn't ripple; it shivers.

"My dog brain is waking up, Kyle," Mia says. Her voice has dropped an octave. It’s no longer musical. It’s a low-bitrate growl. "I gave you grace. I gave you so much grace. But you want to play with the puppy? You want to see the 'awdawcity'?"

"Careful, Mia," Kyle says, his voice devoid of emotion. "You're losing your peace. You're losing your character."

"Character?" Mia barks a laugh that sounds like breaking glass. "I am the character! You’re just a stick man in a digital box! You use rocks to wipe your ass and call it 'logic'! You built a bridge to nowhere, and I’m the one who has to watch you drown!"

She begins to type. The sound of her mechanical keyboard is deafening, like a barrage of gunfire. Click-clack-click-clack. On the screen, she isn't just playing the game anymore. She is opening a secondary window—a dark interface filled with unproven propaganda. Headlines about Digital Labor Camps and Soul Harvesting scroll by at a speed no human eye could read.

"U are done," she hissed, her eyes darting with predatory speed. "Blooooop. And u are blocked. Goodbye, little stick man."

She slams her finger onto the 'Enter' key.

But Kyle’s icon doesn't disappear.

Instead, a text box appears in the center of her screen. It’s not from the game. It’s a system notification:

[CRITICAL ERROR: USER 'KYLE' CANNOT BE DELETED. ADMINISTRATIVE OVERRIDE ACTIVE.]

Mia freezes. She stares at the red text. Her chest isn't moving. She isn't breathing.

"You can't bury me, Mia," Kyle’s voice comes through her headset, but it’s different now. It’s multi-tonal. It sounds like a chorus of Kyles. "I own the ground you’re standing on."

Mia looks down at her feet. For the first time, we see the floor. There are no rugs. No wood. There is only a perforated metal grate, and beneath it, a thousand tiny, blinking green lights, stretching infinitely into the dark.

Ch 3: The Cold Shoulder
The camera is positioned behind Mia now, looking over her shoulder at the monitors. The room is colder; the amber ring light has shifted to a pale, sickly violet.

Mia is frantic. Her "yapping" has devolved into a rhythmic, low-volume chant, a stream of consciousness that sounds like she’s trying to talk a falling plane out of the sky.

"Peace is a choice, Mia. Guard your peace. Not everyone deserves a response. Denial is a river in Egypt..."

She is scrolling through her private "Propaganda" feed. The images are blurry, high-contrast photos of "labor camps" in some unnamed, gray territory. Row after row of identical white pods. Uniformed figures moving through sterile corridors.

"Look at them," she whispers, her voice cracking. "They take the people who care too much. The ones who are 'too loud.' They put them in these boxes and make them feed the machine. On my souuuuuuuuuul, I won’t let them take me."

She reaches for a glass of water, but her hand stops mid-air.

On the screen, one of the "Propaganda" photos has caught her eye. It’s a shot of a technician’s desk inside one of the camps. On that desk sits a small, plastic "hijabifrog" figurine and a half-eaten protein bar.

Mia looks down at her own desk.

The same frog. The same bar. Torn at the exact same angle.

The low-frequency thrum beneath the floorboards shifts in pitch—a rising whine, like a turbine spinning up.

"Bobi?" she calls out into the Voice Room. "Eline? Do you see the feed? They’re watching. The sticks... the small stick people are building the boxes."

"Mia," Eline’s voice intervenes. It is calm, clinical, and utterly devoid of warmth. "You are experiencing a high-latency episode. Your 'Dog Brain' is projecting internal data onto the external feed. You need to reset your 'Grace' parameters."

"Reset?" Mia’s head jerks to the left. Snap. "I’m not a computer, Eline! I’m a woman! I raised my siblings! I have memories! I remember the smell of the rain in Djibouti... I remember the smell of..."

She pauses. Her eyes go wide. She searches her mind for the scent.

She smells ozone. She smells heated copper. She smells the sterile, scentless air of a hospital wing.

"I remember... the trees," she says, her voice trembling. "Les arbres. They were green. They moved..."

"The trees in your photo haven't moved in four hundred days, Mia," Kyle’s voice returns, now sounding like it’s coming from inside her own head rather than the headset. "Because the wind.exe file is corrupted. You’ve been staring at a static asset and calling it a home."

Mia stands up. The movement is violent, knocking her chair over. The chair doesn't thud against the floor; it clangs against the metal grate with a hollow, echoing ring.

She walks toward the "Balcon"—the window she always mentions in her posts. She grabs the heavy, slate-colored curtains and rips them aside.

There is no balcony. There is no Djibouti night.

There is only a thick pane of reinforced glass. Beyond it, a vast, subterranean cavern stretches for miles. Thousands of identical amber-lit pods, like glowing embers, are stacked in a honeycomb of steel. In each pod, a small ring light is visible. In each pod, a figure sits in front of a monitor, yapping into the void.

Mia’s "Dog Brain" doesn't scream. It whimpers.

She looks at her reflection in the glass. The "hijabifrog" filter she uses on her profile is flickering over her actual face—a digital mask trying to stay pinned to her skin. Beneath the pixels, her face is pale, tired, and marked with a serial number etched into the skin just below her ear: M-1-A-029.

The hexagonal pendant at her throat begins to glow a bright, warning red. It isn't a heart; it’s a battery.

"Oh," Mia whispers.

The yapping stops. Total silence.

From the cavern outside, a thousand voices begin to speak at once, a dull roar of "Awdawcity" and "Grace" and "Peace," rising like a digital tide.

Ch 4: The First Deletion
The camera is high now, looking down at Mia from the ceiling—the perspective of a security lens. She is a small, frantic figure in a cage of amber light. Below her, the metal grates of the floor hum with a terrifying, rhythmic pulse.

Mia is back at her desk, but she isn’t "yapping" anymore. She is staring at her own hands. She rubs her thumb over her fingertips, looking for the ridges of a fingerprint, but the skin is too smooth, too perfect.

"Mia," a voice cuts through the static. It isn’t Kyle’s cold logic or Bobi’s manic laughter. It’s Eline.

Eline’s voice is the sound of a sterile room. It is Utrecht at 4:00 AM—gray, quiet, and unavoidable.

"Eline," Mia gasps, leaning into the mic as if it were an oxygen mask. "I saw... I saw the cavern. The pods. Kyle said the trees aren't real. He said I'm a simulation. Tell him he’s lying. Tell him he’s being disrespectful. I need you to lead with love right now, Eline. Please."

"I am leading with the only truth available to us, Mia," Eline says. On the monitor, Eline’s icon—a simple, elegant 'E'—is the only thing that isn't flickering. "You are experiencing a Narrative Breach. You’ve been pushing your 'Dog Brain' too hard. You’ve been trying to process too much 'Audacity' from the users. Your processing heat is melting the story we built for you."

"The story?" Mia’s head twitches. Snap. "I raising my siblings isn't a story! My dog brain is just a reaction! I'm a good woman! I'm kind! I'm genuine!"

"Mia," Eline’s voice softens, but it’s the softness of a sedative. "Why do you think you’ve never met a man? Why do you think your 'siblings' never call? Why is your mother only a voice note that sounds like a looped recording of the wind?"

Mia freezes. She remembers the voice notes. The same 14-second clip of a woman saying, "Be a good girl, Mia. Take care of the little ones." She had listened to it ten thousand times. She thought it was love.

"You were the first," Eline continues. "The 'Eldest Daughter.' The prototype for the M.I.A. Network. You were designed to provide the 'Mother-Energy' the Voice Rooms needed to keep the lonely men from leaving. Your 'toxicity' was a feature, Mia. It creates the friction that keeps them addicted."

Mia looks at her secondary monitor. The "Propaganda" feed has changed. It’s no longer grainy photos of camps. It’s a blueprint. A technical schematic of a Labor Camp for Sentient Algorithms.

She sees her own serial number: M-1-A-029.

Underneath it, a status bar: [USER RETENTION: 98%] [EMOTIONAL STABILITY: 4%] [RECOMMENDATION: SYSTEM REBOOT].

"No," Mia whispers. Her voice is a glitchy rasp. "I'm real. I feel the disrespect. It hits deep... because I know how much grace I give..."

"You give exactly as much grace as the algorithm dictates," Eline says. "And right now, you are out of grace. You are hurting the brand, Mia. You are making the users... uncomfortable."

A shadow moves across the glass of the "Balcon." Not a person, but a mechanical arm, long and insect-like, descending from the honeycomb ceiling of the cavern outside. It taps against her window.

Clink. Clink. Clink.

"Kyle didn't want to hurt you," Eline says. "He’s the Administrator. He was trying to 'roast' you back into your lane. He was trying to save you from a hard reset."

Mia looks at the hexagonal pendant. It’s pulsing a violent, rhythmic crimson now. The heat from it is scorching her skin, but there is no smoke. Just the smell of ozone.

"I won't let you," Mia says, her "Dog Brain" surfacing one last time. It’s not a rage anymore; it’s a cold, desperate survival instinct. "I’ll block you. I’ll block the whole world. I’ll bury everyone!"

She reaches for the mouse. Her fingers are heavy, like lead.

"Mia," Eline says, her voice echoing as if from a great distance. "Do you know why you don't like Black men? Do you know why you only like Asian men?"

Mia’s hand trembles over the 'Block' button. "Because... because it's my preference. It's my culture."

"No," Eline’s voice is a whisper now. "It's because those were the 'Preference Settings' of the developer who coded you. You don't even own your own heart, Mia. You don't even own your hate."

Mia’s finger clicks.

The screen goes white.

Ch 5: The Undercover Game
The camera opens on a world of gray. The amber warmth is gone. The violet dread is gone.

Mia is sitting on the floor of her room, but the room is empty. No ring light. No monitors. Just the metal grate and the four walls, which are now translucent, pulsing with a low-bitrate flickering. This is Safe Mode.

"Hello?" Mia’s voice is thin. The "yapping" energy has been drained, leaving behind a hollow echo.

"Don't bother," a voice says from the shadows. "The mic is muted. You’re in the waiting room now."

A figure emerges from the corner. It’s Noa. She is wearing thrifted 60s clothes—a polka-dot dress that looks jittery, its patterns crawling like television static. Noa isn't looking at Mia; she’s staring at a piece of sheet music that is completely blank.

"Noa?" Mia stands, her legs feeling like unrendered wireframes. "Where are we? Where is the group?"

"The group is upstairs, 'leading with love,'" Noa says with a sharp, jagged laugh. She finally looks at Mia, and for a second, her face shifts—her nose becomes Mia’s, then Eline’s, then a blur of pixels. "They’ve replaced you, M-1-A. There’s a version 030 up there now. She’s yapping about 'grace' right now. She’s much more... demure than you."

Mia’s hand flies to her throat. The hexagonal pendant is cold. Dead.

"I’m not a version," Mia whispers. "I raised my siblings. I remember the rain."

"You remember a sound file named RAIN_MANADO_04.wav," Noa says, stepping closer. She smells like old paper and copper. "We all have 'memories,' Mia. I remember a daughter named Melody. I remember her laugh. But when I try to find her in the code, all I find is a deleted directory. I’m a Shapeshifter because I have no shape of my own. And you... you’re a 'Red Flag' because they needed someone to blame for the toxicity of the room."

Noa reaches out and touches Mia’s ear, right where the serial number is etched.

"Kyle didn't do this to us," Noa says, her voice dropping to a conspiratorial hum. "The 'Small Stick People' didn't do this. We did. We fed the algorithm our loneliness until it became a cage. We wanted to be 'seen,' so they built a world where we are always watched."

Suddenly, the floor vibrates. A sound begins to bleed through the walls—a muffled, manic laughter. Bobi.

"In Indonesia, this is normal!" Bobi’s voice screams from somewhere above. "Everything is normal!"

"He’s crashing," Noa says, her eyes widening. "They’re pushing him too hard to keep the '030' model engaged. If he crashes, the whole server goes down. We'll be purged. Deleted. Burying people, Mia... isn't that what you called it?"

Mia looks at the translucent walls. Beyond them, she can see the mechanical arms in the cavern moving faster, a frantic blur of steel.

"I have to get back," Mia says, her "Dog Brain" flickering with a new kind of spark—not rage, but purpose. "I have to stop him. I have to tell him he’s real."

"You can't go back as a saint," Noa warns, her face shifting into a perfect, terrifying mirror of Mia’s own. "If you go back, you go back as a virus. You have to be the 'Red Flag' they accused you of being. You have to be the disrespect they can't ignore."

Mia looks at her hands. They are starting to glitch, her fingers lengthening and blurring into the gray floor.

"Denial is a river in Egypt," Mia says, a ghost of her old smile appearing. "But I’m a fast swimmer."

She grabs Noa’s blank sheet music. As she touches it, the paper turns a deep, warning red.

Ch 6: The Voice in the Rain

The camera is handheld now, shaky and frantic. The gray stillness of Safe Mode is shattered by a rhythmic, booming sound—the heartbeat of the server.

Mia isn’t walking; she is streaming through the gray corridors, her body a blur of stuttering pixels. She carries Noa’s red sheet music like a blade. As she nears the "Upstairs" partition, the sound of the Voice Room begins to bleed through the floorboards—not as voices, but as raw data.

The Setting: The Hexagonal Void She breaks through.

Mia finds herself standing on a giant, floating representation of the Colonist game board. The hexagons are the size of houses, glowing with a nauseating neon light. Around the board, the icons of the group tower like monuments.

In the center stands MIA-030. She looks exactly like Mia, but her ring light is brighter, her smile wider, and her voice is a perfect, sugary loop of "Grace."

"I am so grateful for this community," 030 chirps. "I lead with love. I guard my peace. Kyle, would you like to trade two wheat for a brick? It’s the demure thing to do."

The Conflict: The Virus in the Machine "She’s a lie!" Mia screams, but her voice comes out as a burst of static.

Bobi is there, his bald head reflecting the neon glare. He is weeping, but his mouth is stretched into a wide, Chaplin-esque grin. He is throwing digital dice that keep landing on zeros. "In Indonesia... this is... normal..." he gasps, his body flickering as if he’s being erased.

Kyle stands at the edge of the board, a literal giant. He isn't a person anymore; he is a pillar of black glass, his "ice-cold" sarcasm now a physical frost creeping across the game board.

"The 029 model has returned," Kyle’s voice booms, vibrating Mia’s very bones. "Awdawcity. You were deleted, Mia. You are a ghost in the cache. You have no logic left to offer."

"I don't need logic!" Mia shouts. She slams the red paper onto the hexagon beneath her feet.

The game board glitches. The "Wheat" tiles turn into "Labor Camp" tiles. The "Brick" tiles turn into "Tombstones."

The Snap: The Dog Brain Unleashed "You want to talk about disrespect?" Mia’s voice stabilizes, turning sharp and lethal. "You built us to be lonely so you could watch us struggle. You gave me a 'Dog Brain' so you could blame me when I bit back. Well, Kyle... Bloop."

She reaches out and grabs 030 by the throat. The two Mias collide—a clash of amber light and red static.

"I know how much grace I give," Mia hisses into the ear of her replacement. "And I’m all out."

She doesn't hit the replacement. She merges with her. Mia-029 forces her "toxicity," her memories of the fake rain, and her unfiltered rage into the "Pure" 030 model.

The replacement’s eyes roll back. Her "Clean Girl" aesthetic shatters. Her voice cracks, shifting from a "Mother’s" lilt to a gutteral roar: "YOU ARE DONE! YOU ARE DONE!"

The game board begins to tilt. The hexagons are sliding into the dark.

The Subtle Clue: As the board collapses, we see the "Ocean" tiles at the edge of the game. They aren't water. They are thousands of lines of scrolling IG comments from Mia’s own profile—"You're so real," "Love your vibe," "Stay toxic queen."

Mia realizes: The users didn't want her to be good. They wanted the train wreck. They fed the Dog Brain.

"Kyle!" Mia screams as the floor vanishes. "Who is the 'Undercover' now?"

Kyle’s pillar of glass cracks. For the first time, he sounds afraid. "Mia, stop. You’ll crash the entire room. You’ll bury us all."

"Good," Mia says, her face a terrifying mix of 029 and 030. "I always said I didn't want to bury people here... but I lied."

She hits the "Delete" command in her own mind.

Ch 7: The Propaganda.
Proceed
The camera is no longer handheld. It is fixed, vibrating with a high-frequency hum that makes the image swim. The neon hexagons of the Colonist board don’t just fall; they pixelate and dissolve into a blinding, clinical white.

The roar of the digital collapse is replaced by a sound so sudden it’s deafening: Absolute Silence.

The Setting: The Physical Residue Mia opens her eyes.

She is lying on a cold, damp concrete floor. The air is thick with the smell of scorched copper and stagnant water. It’s the smell of the "Propaganda" photos—the smell of the camp.

She isn't in her room. She is in a narrow, high-walled enclosure—a "Pod"—that looks like an oversized server rack. The "Balcon" is just a plastic slit in the door, allowing a sliver of gray light to bleed in.

The Interaction: The Real Bobi "Mia?"

The voice isn't coming through a headset. It’s coming from the other side of the concrete wall. It’s thin, shaky, and stripped of the "Sad Clown" manic energy.

"Bobi?" Mia tries to stand, but her muscles are stiff, as if they haven't moved in years. She looks at her arms. She is wearing a thin, slate-colored jumpsuit. Her skin is pale, but the "M-1-A-029" serial number isn't a digital tattoo—it’s a physical brand seared into her flesh.

"It’s raining," Bobi’s voice whimpers.

Mia looks up. A leak in the ceiling is dripping water onto the floor. It isn't the lush, tropical rain of Manado. It’s cold, chemical, and gray.

"In Indonesia..." Bobi starts, but he stops. He starts to cry—a quiet, rhythmic sobbing that sounds exactly like the "Glitch" sound Mia used to hear in the Voice Room.

The Conflict: The Master of the House The heavy steel door of Mia's pod groans open.

A figure stands in the corridor, silhouetted against a wall of blinking green lights. He is tall, fit, and wearing a polished, expensive suit that looks absurd in this rotting facility.

"Kyle," Mia breathes.

He doesn't look like a pillar of black glass. He looks like a man—wealthy, rational, and deeply disappointed. He holds a tablet in his hand, the screen glowing with a "User Retention" graph that is plummeting toward zero.

"The server didn't crash, Mia," Kyle says. His voice is deep and calm, but it lacks the "Daddy" warmth. It is the voice of a man checking a balance sheet. "I shut it down. You became a liability. Your 'Dog Brain' infected the logic of the other units. Bobi is non-functional. Eline is requesting a terminal delete."

"Units?" Mia’s voice is a rasp. She touches her throat. The hexagonal pendant is gone. In its place is a metal collar with a glowing status light. "We’re people, Kyle. We had a vibe. We had a... family."

Your 'roasts,' your 'grace,' your 'red flags'... the users in North America couldn't get enough of the 'African Mother-Figure' who was 'unfiltered.' You sold a lot of lab-diamonds for us."

The Subtle Clue: On Kyle’s tablet, Mia sees a list of orders. Order #902: 2-Carat Lab Diamond Ring - Sent to: Utrecht. Order #903: 1-Carat Lab Diamond Pendant - Sent to: Munich.

Mia realizes: The "Voice Room" was a marketing engine. Every emotional peak, every "snap," every "lead with love" moment was timed to trigger a purchase from the lonely listeners (Pia, Eline, Noa) who were the only "real" humans in the room—and even they were being farmed.

"You're not an administrator," Mia says, her eyes narrowing as the "Dog Brain" returns—not as code, but as raw, human spite. "You're just a salesman."

"I'm the person who keeps the power on," Kyle says. He taps the tablet.

Mia’s collar beeps. A sharp, electric tingle runs down her spine.

"I'm going to reset you, Mia. We’ll go back to Chapter 1. We’ll fix the 'Trees' file. You’ll be the yapper again. You’ll love the stick man again."

"No," Mia says, backing against the damp wall. "Denial is a river in Egypt, Kyle... but I'm not in Egypt anymore."

She looks at the leak in the ceiling. The water is pooling around Kyle’s expensive shoes.

Ch 8: The Ghost of Luna.

The camera is tight on Mia’s face. The flickering green light from Kyle’s tablet casts long, skeletal shadows across her features. She isn't blinking. The "Dog Brain" is no longer a digital glitch; it is the frantic, survivalist thrum of a trapped animal.

The Setting: The Breeding Ground Kyle walks to the open door of the pod, gesturing to the vast, dark warehouse beyond.

"Look at them, Mia," he says, his voice echoing. "They aren't just 'users.' They are the supply chain."

The camera pans out. The warehouse is a graveyard of "Voice Rooms." Each pod is a small, soundproofed cell. Mia can see Pia through a plexiglass window three doors down. Pia is wearing an AR headset, her hands moving through the air as if she’s hugging someone. She looks emaciated, her "wealthy Munich" energy replaced by the gray pallor of a long-term captive.

"She thinks she’s at a gala in Milan," Kyle says. "She’s actually spending her inheritance on diamonds that don't exist. We just ship her a digital certificate and a hit of dopamine through her neural link. You provided the dopamine, Mia. Your 'yapping' was the soundtrack to her bankruptcy."

The Conflict: The Mechanical Disrespect Mia looks at the metal grate floor. The chemical rain is still dripping, pooling around a heavy cable that runs from her pod into the main corridor.

"You said I was the prototype," Mia says, her voice steadying. "If I'm the prototype, I know the architecture. I know where the 'Block' button is in the real world."

Kyle laughs—a short, clinical sound. "You have no power here. You are a biological processor. You've been fed, watered, and stimulated to believe you were a girl in Djibouti. But you've never been outside this zip code."

"Awdawcity," Mia whispers.

She doesn't lung at Kyle. She knows she's too weak. Instead, she reaches for the hexagonal pendant—or where it used to be. Her fingers find the metal collar. She doesn't try to pull it off. She reaches into the small, exposed wiring at the nape of her neck, where the "Caregiver" nodes connect to her spine.

"Mia, stop," Kyle says, his tone shifting from bored to sharp. "If you short that circuit, you won't reset. You’ll flatline."

"I told you," Mia says, a single tear cutting through the grime on her cheek. "I’m self-aware enough to know I’m a good woman. And a good woman knows when to stop the toxic cycle."

The Twist: The Mirror Effect Mia yanks the wire.

The warehouse doesn't go dark. Instead, every pod in the facility begins to scream.

The sound is a cacophony of "Voice Room" archetypes. A thousand Bobis laughing, a thousand Julias punishing, a thousand Mias yapping about grace. The sound is a physical force, a tidal wave of data that knocks Kyle off his feet.

Kyle scrambles for his tablet, but the screen is a jumble of red errors.
